Does the EU AI Act apply to my company?

Probably yes — if you build, sell, or use an AI system and it affects people in the EU, even if your company sits outside the EU. The Act reaches beyond EU borders by design Article 2.

It covers providers (who develop and place AI on the market) and deployers (who use AI in their work). It doesn't matter whether you built the AI or bought it — using it can create duties. Narrow exemptions exist for purely personal use and most military, defence and national-security uses.

Is my HR / hiring AI tool high-risk?

Usually yes. AI used for recruitment and selection — screening CVs, filtering or ranking candidates, targeted job ads — and for work decisions like promotion, termination, task allocation, and monitoring or evaluating performance is listed as high-risk Annex III(4).

High-risk means real duties: human oversight, transparency to candidates, accuracy and bias controls, logging, and registration. Separately, AI that infers emotions in the workplace is prohibited Article 5, outside narrow medical or safety uses. And even if a vendor built the tool, you're the deployer and carry your own duties Article 26.

Is my edtech / education app high-risk?

Often yes. AI is high-risk when it decides access or admission to education, evaluates learning outcomes (including steering how someone learns), assesses the appropriate level of education, or monitors and detects prohibited behaviour during tests — i.e. proctoring Annex III(3).

Lighter tools — a grammar helper, a content recommender — usually aren't high-risk. Emotion-recognition in educational settings is prohibited Article 5. The label "edtech" doesn't decide it; the specific use does.

Provider vs deployer — which am I?

A provider develops an AI system (or has one developed) and places it on the market or into service under its own name Article 3(3) — and carries the heaviest obligations Article 16.

A deployer uses an AI system in a professional context Article 3(4) — lighter but real duties: human oversight, using it as instructed, monitoring, and informing affected people Article 26.

The catch: a deployer can become a provider — by putting its name on a high-risk system, substantially modifying it, or changing its purpose Article 25. Agencies building AI for clients are usually providers; companies using a bought tool are usually deployers.

What do I need to do before 2 August 2026?

2 August 2026 is when most obligations start applying — including the high-risk requirements for Annex III systems and the Article 50 transparency duties Article 113. (Prohibited practices have applied since 2 Feb 2025; general-purpose AI rules since 2 Aug 2025; some product-embedded high-risk systems have until 2 Aug 2027.)

Your practical first steps:

1 — Inventory every AI system you build or use.
2 — Classify each one: prohibited, high-risk, limited, or minimal.
3 — For high-risk, close the gaps: human oversight, documentation, data governance, transparency, logging, registration.
4 — For chatbots and AI-generated content, add the Article 50 disclosures.

What are the EU AI Act fines?

Three tiers Article 99:

• Up to €35M or 7% of total worldwide annual turnover (whichever is higher) — for using prohibited AI practices.
• Up to €15M or 3% — for breaching most other obligations, including high-risk and transparency duties.
• Up to €7.5M or 1% — for giving incorrect or misleading information to authorities.

For SMEs and start-ups, the lower of the fixed sum or the percentage applies. In practice, the bigger cost is usually operational and reputational — finding out mid-crisis instead of in advance.
