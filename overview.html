Start from zero

Never heard of it? Here's the whole thing in one breath.

The EU AI Act is the world's first real rulebook for artificial intelligence. It doesn't ban AI. It sorts every AI system by how much harm it could do — and the more it can hurt someone, the more you have to prove it's safe, honest, and under human control.

If your product, your team, or even your vendor touches AI and reaches anyone in the EU, it applies to you — wherever in the world you're based.

How it thinks

Four levels. That's the whole trick.

UnacceptableBanned outright — social scoring, manipulation, reading emotions at work.
High riskAllowed, but tightly controlled — hiring, credit, health, education, policing.
LimitedJust be honest — chatbots and deepfakes must say they're AI.
MinimalFree to go — spam filters, game AI, the everyday stuff.

Almost every fight about compliance is really a fight about which level you're in. That's where I come in.

Why it matters

This isn't paperwork. It's people.

Amazon built an AI to screen CVs. It taught itself to penalise the word "women's." They scrapped it — but only after it had been quietly filtering people out.

Bias doesn't shout. It scales.

Robert Williams was arrested in front of his daughters. A facial-recognition match was wrong. He spent 30 hours in a cell — for someone else's face.

When AI is wrong, a real person pays.

An airline's chatbot invented a refund policy. A tribunal made the airline honour it. "The bot said it" was not a defence.

You own what your AI says.

The Act exists because these stop being headlines and start being Tuesdays.

Most HR teams already run AI they didn't build — CV screeners, sourcing tools, "culture-fit" scorers — and assume the vendor carries the risk. You don't, fully. You're the deployer, with your own duties.

Recruitment and people-decision AI is high-risk under the Act. And AI that reads emotions in the workplace? That's outright banned.

  • Inventory every people-facing AI tool you touch.
  • Pin down your deployer duties: human oversight, candidate transparency, bias checks.
  • Vendor due-diligence, so you're not quietly holding their liability.

You build AI for clients and assume the client owns the compliance. Usually the contract is silent — and the Act puts the duty on whoever builds and ships it. That's you: the "provider."

Article 50 transparency alone — disclosure, labelling, documentation — lands on the builder. Most teams discover this only after they've shipped.

  • Split provider vs deployer cleanly, per client engagement.
  • Gap-check everything against the Aug 2026 deadline.
  • Contract language that puts the risk back where it belongs.
